## Every Project That Won a YC Interview at a Hackathon

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Y Combinator has been offering guaranteed partner interviews as hackathon prizes since its first hackathon (YC Hacks) in 2014. The strategy has proven remarkably effective as a deal-flow mechanism, with several hackathon winners going on to build companies worth hundreds of millions or billions of dollars. The most striking examples include Athelas (healthcare diagnostics, YC S16), Kalshi (prediction markets, now a $2B+ exchange), Soshi (AI social media, $138K ARR within a year), April (voice email AI, YC S25), and Camfer (AI CAD, $4.8M raised).

The pattern across all winning projects is consistent: judges reward projects that (1) solve a real, specific problem, (2) demonstrate technical depth beyond a surface-level demo, (3) show clear commercial potential as a startup, and (4) are presented with a compelling live demo rather than slides. YC partners have explicitly stated they look for "earnestness over polish," "first principles thinking," and founders who "make something people want."

### 1. YC HACKS (August 2-3, 2014) -- Y Combinator's FIRST Hackathon

**Event Details:**
- Location: YC Office, Mountain View
- Duration: ~24 hours
- Prize: YC interview for winners

**GRAND PRIZE WINNER: Athelas**
- What they built: A smartphone accessory using a $5 ball lens taped to an iPod Touch camera that could photograph blood samples and automatically detect malaria through computer vision
- Technology: iOS app, computer vision, low-cost optics (ball lens), image classification
- Team: Tanay Tandon (solo -- 17 years old, Cupertino High School student)
- What made judges pick them: A 17-year-old walked on stage with an iPod, a toilet roll, and medical slides and demonstrated live malaria detection. The combination of extreme simplicity (a $5 lens), massive potential impact (malaria kills hundreds of thousands annually), and technical sophistication was irresistible.
- Did they get into YC? YES -- Athelas was accepted to YC S16
- What happened after: Athelas pivoted from malaria detection to general blood diagnostics. The company now sells FDA-approved devices that detect immune cell counts from blood samples in minutes. Tandon went on to found Commure, a healthcare infrastructure company. This is THE canonical example of "hackathon to unicorn pipeline."
- Build time: Overnight (~12-16 hours)

**Other Notable Winners:**

- Savant (3rd place)

## SECTION 2: WHAT YC PARTNERS SAY ABOUT HACKATHON JUDGING

### Garry Tan (YC President & CEO)

**On what YC looks for:**
- "The reason why startups don't work is they don't make something people want, either making the wrong thing or not making anything at all."
- Looks for "first principles thinkers -- those who not only believe what no one believed yet, but figure out what the necessary things are to build"
- Wants founders who "can create a market, that they see a technology no one has envisioned yet"
- Looks for "a fringe thing" -- unconventional ideas with hidden potential
- Champions "ambitious misfits" and values "earnestness over polish"
- Emphasizes that AI has transformed demo impact: "people now look at a demo and say 'it's really impressive... I need it right now'"

**On hackathon-to-startup:**
- Tan acknowledges hackathon projects as valid starting points but stresses: "where the rubber hits the road is you actually go and talk to people and show them the demo"
- The demo needs to demonstrate real user need, not just technical cleverness

### Riley Shu (Multiple YC/AI Hackathon Winner -- Participant Perspective)

**Core winning principles from someone who won first place at YC and OpenAI hackathons:**
1. "Strong idea = useful + unexpected solution" -- Ideas should solve real problems from personal experience
2. Small teams outperform: "one coder, one generalist, and one support" -- minimize communication overhead
3. "Demo-first development approach" -- avoid slides, focus on live demonstrations with clear UX and "wow effect"
4. Networking during hackathons matters for visibility

## SECTION 3: PATTERN ANALYSIS -- WHAT WINS

### Common Traits of Winning Projects

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| Solves a SPECIFIC real problem | Athelas (malaria), Vera Health (clinical search), Camfer (CAD design), April (email while driving) |

| Clear path to becoming a company | Kalshi (prediction markets), Soshi (SaaS), Camfer (enterprise CAD), April (consumer subscription) |
| Founder-problem fit | Vera Health founders had physician parents, Gecko Sec founder was ex-NCSC security engineer |

### What Does NOT Win

- Projects that are "cool demos" without commercial viability
- Overly complicated explanations (Michael Seibel: "use the simplest language")
- Slide-heavy presentations without live demos
- Large teams with coordination overhead
- Projects that are thin wrappers around APIs without novel value

### Typical Build Time

Most YC hackathons are 24-36 hours. Winners build functional MVPs in this window.
